Workbook

Interactive Media by Madina Ganieva

Week II

Research

Anaiis by OB STUDIO

anaiis.world can be described as a type of experimental portfolio where the artwork of the artist is showcased in a very visual and non-linear manner. Rather than using the traditional approach to layout, anaiis.world makes use of layers, emotional typography, and unusual navigation techniques that represent the personality of the artist.

The unique thing about the website is its focus on the expression of the artist over functionality. The website seems closer to an interactive experience with a piece of art as opposed to a regular portfolio, thus showing the web designing possibilities beyond simple information presentation.

BRUNO SIMON

The Bruno Simon website bruno-simon.com can actually be considered to be a gaming web-application, presented as a portfolio, with users having the opportunity to move around a 3D environment by means of controlling a car. As it seems quite interesting, what should be noted is that this is an innovative approach to developing a portfolio due to the fact that the website represents a gaming project with unique features. Thus, it was created with the use of technologies such as 3D and WebGL which proves the technical level of the developer. Nevertheless, what is even more valuable about this design is the fact that websites do not always need to conform to the principles of user experience.

Jules Esick Photographer

julesesick.com seems like an interactive portfolio site for a creative individual with simple designs, and subtle animations and transitions. Not very innovative in terms of design, the site still finds a nice middle ground in between creativity and usability. One interesting feature about this website is how it has focused on details like animations and fonts; small interactions which increase user satisfaction. This is definitely one example of a realistic take on creative web design.

Workshop

In this particular workshop, the idea was to brainstorm rapidly on interfaces through the process called "Crazy 8s", considering various possibilities for the use of websites like workbooks, other than typical webpage designs. The session challenged participants to consider websites not just as spaces, but also in terms of narratives or interactions, using visual metaphors like books, slideshows, clocks, flowers, and cityscapes. In the pin-up session, we discussed the implications of interface structure for user interaction with information.

For the prototype paper designing session, I used my "city skyscrapers" idea. This interface design visualizes the workbook at night time as the city, where windows are lit up as clickable projects/experiments that the person can visit. Once clicked on a lighted-up window, the camera moves to the particular apartment/room where there is the content of the project within.

Week III

Research

BP&O

bpando.org operates as a selected collection of design projects, especially those dealing with branding and identity design. The site features case studies that contain visuals and commentary, thus proving to be inspiring and informative. The most valuable thing about bpando.org is its editing style, since it does not merely collect projects but interprets and analyzes them, distinguishing itself from other galleries.

Hoverstat

Hoverstat.es is an experimental archive of website design that collects experimental web designs and emphasizes innovation and uniqueness. Hoverstat.es focuses on web designs that experiment with both interactions and visuals, instead of concentrating on usability. It stands out because it serves as a selection tool, showing the user only what’s good.

The Pudding

Pudding Cool is an online database containing numerous essays in which cultural and social phenomena are analyzed using design, journalism, and interactivity. The peculiarity of this project lies in the interactive features and data visualisation, transforming the traditional archive into a highly engaging and educational tool for the user.

Workshop

The current workshop focuses on utilizing the Google Stitch technology as a means for quickly turning hand-drawn sketch into an interface using artificial intelligence-driven generation. I uploaded my "skyscraper city view" sketch produced during the Crazy 8s workshop onto the platform in order to see what kind of an interface would be created as a result. The first output resulted in a sophisticated corporate landing page with a dark gradient background featuring minimalistic typography which could loosely remind the image of a city skyline at sunset.

Next, I tried to modify my prompts in a way that would lead to an interface which looks like a hand-drawn one with illuminated yellow windows representing glowing apartment lights. The second output proved to be a much better fit for my original concept and metaphor.

Week IV

Research

Chloé: See with Optimism by Dalziel & Pow

See with Optimism by Chloé is an interactive installation designed by Dalziel & Pow in partnership with Variable.io and illustrator Cecilia Carlstedt, to promote Chloé’s eyewear collection. Using face tracking and generative animation, the installation turns the user’s reflection into a digital portrait consisting of moving brush strokes and changing colours that respond in real time to motion and interaction.

The most significant thing about this installation is the way it uses technology not just as an interface but as a way to generate emotions and atmosphere. Rather than displaying a static set of images, the project creates an immersive experience in which the viewer becomes the subject of art. This project is an example of a new approach to interactive media and experiential design based on using computer systems to generate participation, emotions, and engagement.

Workshop

The workshop exposed us to basics of programming in p5.js and creative coding, playing with shapes, colours, layers, and animation. I discovered ways in which basic shapes and structures could be played around with using functions, looping, variables, and cursor interaction. One of the key exercises of the workshop was to create visual forms using text, alphabets, and paths that would interact based on position.

The exercise helped me realize how the world of generative visuals operates based on behaviours and rules. I was fascinated by how even basic code can result in very complicated and chaotic images when used repetitively. Coding also began to take on a new significance for me as not just a technical exercise, but a creative and visual one that allowed interaction and setting of atmosphere. This is closely tied in with my later project concepts of interactive geometry and sensory experiences.

Week V

Research

Data Heartbreak by Domestic Data Streamers

The Data Heartbreak installation, created by Domestic Data Streamers, transforms anonymous stories of heartbreaks into visual and spatial experiences of data. Unlike conventional approaches to data visualisation, which tend to treat information objectively or statistically, Data Heartbreak reinterprets the meaning of data as something highly emotional and highly human. Domestic Data Streamers are known for their projects that combine data visualisation, design, and social research to make human experiences more tangible and emotionally engaging.

The key strength of this project is that it makes invisible emotional experiences visible and participatory. What I find most inspiring about this project is its ability to use computational technologies to convey emotions and atmosphere, rather than function alone, which is in line with my personal interest in transforming sensory experiences into visual systems.

Dear Data by Giorgia Lupi & Stefanie Posavec

“Dear Data” is a year-long collaboration between Giorgia Lupi and Stefanie Posavec in which both designers have sent each other hand-drawn postcards visualizing personal data collected from their daily lives. This project became very influential in the field of contemporary information design, as it completely changes the perception of data visualization as something impersonal and mechanical into a much more personal, emotional, and even artistic experience through rhythm, repetition, color, and pattern.

This project has been exhibited worldwide and even published as a book, thus becoming an extremely important reference point for both graphic design and data storytelling. What particularly inspired me about this project is the ability of visual systems and repetition to convey emotional atmosphere and experience, which is directly related to my interest in geometry and rhythm.

Indikator by Anders Hellsten Nissen

Indikator is an interactive sonic bench made by Anders Hellsten Nissen and designed by Can Touch This Studio. This project converts touch into sound response and invites participants to interact with the public furniture in an engaging manner. What is interesting about this project is the way that it approaches the notion of interaction itself. Rather than being a system of interaction, this project presents interaction as an experiential process.

In this sense, Indikator creates opportunities for people to engage with each other and explore the public space through auditory interaction and curiosity. Can Touch This Studio is a studio which designs interactive experiences by combining elements from design, play and technology. I was really fascinated with the idea of cross-sensory interaction where interaction with the sense of touch results in the creation of sound.

Workshop

In this workshop, we investigated the connection between visual communication, logic, and computational thinking by engaging in a collaborative drawing project. To begin, we made a rudimentary drawing with the use of basic shapes and then wrote down the instructions for replicating that drawing using solely mathematical and spatial terms like axes, shapes, measures, positioning, and direction. We exchanged instructions with another student in order to replicate the drawings from only the instructions provided.

It became clear through the task how difficult it is to describe a concept visually within logic and how important it is to convey an idea accurately while working with machines through coding language. Additionally, it was revealed that the way we code is not dissimilar from prompting a machine, where any vagueness can lead to entirely different results due to the nature of programming.

Week VI

Research

Ironhill by Uncommon, Julien R., Oli, Diana M.

Ironhill is a digital experience that aims to immerse its user in the world of the Australian architecture studio Ironhill. The collaboration between Uncommon, Julien Renau, Oli, and Diana Marcela brought about a cinematic transition, motion graphics, typography, and interaction that results in a highly atmospheric web experience. It does not operate like any traditional architecture portfolio but acts more like a spatial and immersive web experience, where movement and geometry define architectural identity in the digital sphere.

Ironhill was awarded at Awwwards due to its unique interaction and visual design. However, what fascinated me about the project was how minimal geometric forms and motion systems helped in creating an emotional presence and atmosphere.

Kira Muratova’s Vers Libre by Elena Saharova

Vers Libre is an experimental digital archive and website made by Elena Saharova to investigate the work and visual language of the filmmaker Kira Muratova. Using fragmented compositions, layering of typography, cinematic transitions, and motion systems, the website captures the emotional and visual complexity of the films rather than acting as a typical archive.

The website turns the digital space into a cinematic experience and creates an atmosphere that makes the interaction feel emotional and expressive. What is fascinating about this work is the ability to convey the mood, memories, and narratives through composition and motion rather than explanation. I found myself highly inspired by this work because of the elements of rhythm, fragmentation, and visual atmosphere, which are related to my own investigations of emotional and sensory space through computational visual systems.

Workshop

The first workshop provided a foundation of knowledge of Arduino and physical computing based on the interactions between digital programming and physical components, as well as inputs from the real world. The way of creating a simple circuit using breadboard, jumpers, LEDs, sensors, and outputs was explored. For starters, we created a simple circuit using an LED light that illuminated when receiving signals from digital programming.

To build further on that simple circuit, we used speakers and added a distance sensor to create reactions in sound depending on the object's closeness to the device. If an object approached the sensor, the sound would change its frequency. Overall, I found the workshop useful for gaining an understanding of physical computing as a form of interaction between digital code and real-world input, as well as the notion of responsive environments.

Week VII

Research

Mais où ? by Ruby-Maude Rioux

Mais où? is an audiovisual performance by Ruby-Maude Rioux that consists of improvised piano playing together with real-time generated visuals made in TouchDesigner. This project examines the connection between sounds, images, and movements through the use of responsive computing and generates a synesthetic experience where the music creates the visual mood and spatial movement.

Unlike decorative projections, the visuals in this project have an emotional connection to the performance and continue to change through sounds and rhythms. Ruby-Maude Rioux is known for her works that include performance, generative systems, and immersive media to create poetic sensory experiences. This project inspired me to experiment with translating sounds to visual behavior in geometric systems.

Nippori Seminar by Iamm_tokyo, Shun_kudo, Sora S.

Nippori Seminar is a unique experiment in interactive web page made by Iamm_tokyo, Shun Kudo, and Sora Shimada. This project makes use of unusual layout, typographic elements, geometry, and motion systems to deliver an expressive and immersive web-based experience. Instead of adhering to usual website design principles, Nippori Seminar focuses on atmosphere, rhythm, and exploration, giving the interaction a performance-like quality.

The project received attention on Awwwards due to its extremely experimental approach to web design. I was particularly impressed by the use of geometry and movement as tools for creating emotional and sensory engagement in a digital environment, which directly relates to my interest in interactive computational space.

Workshop

In this project, using Arduino, we developed a proximity detector for wearing on oneself that reacts whenever any object comes within the specified distance of the detector. By programming, we could specify the distance of the object and could connect the sensor with an output device to let the user know about nearby objects. My partner in this project was Deeksha, and we attached our circuit on a paper bag worn around the head to see if the user can navigate without visual cues.

We found out that wearing the sensor around the head was ineffective since the sensor could detect only those objects located at head level, and we would bump into objects located under the head-level plane. Then we redesigned our product, attaching it to the belt instead of the head. In this way, our product started working well since now it could detect the objects near us.

Week VIII

Research

Smell of Data by Leanne Wijnsma

“Smell of Data” is a research project by Leanne Wijnsma that examines the connection between smell, technology, and data. This project explores whether there is a way to experience invisible technologies through smells, instead of visuals, and proposes a different approach for experiencing information through sensory perception.

What makes this project especially interesting is the examination of cross-sensory interaction and synaesthesia, thus questioning the primacy of the visual interface used in digital technologies. The translation of data into smell creates an embodiment of the interaction with technology. This project was especially inspirational for me because of its approach to sensory translation, which resonates well with my interest in the transformation of sounds into visual geometry.

Talking Popcorn by Nina Katchadourian

“Talking Popcorn” is an audio and video art piece by Nina Katchadourian in which popping popcorn is presented to have a form of dialogue using synchronized sound effects and subtitles. The artist creates a dialogue between popping sounds of popcorn using editing techniques that make the sounds resemble a conversation. The piece encourages the audience to interpret sounds as conversation, emotions, and personality traits.

“Talking Popcorn” is an interesting piece because of the way in which the artist explores interpretation, perception, and sound associations. Katchadourian is well-known for her ability to turn everyday items and situations into witty pieces of conceptual art that explore human communication and behavior. In particular, I found the piece intriguing because of the way in which it creates emotional and poetic moments from sound interactions that is related to my own interests in translating sound into visuals.

Workshop

This workshop experimented with non-traditional input methods and explored new sound interaction techniques using Arduino and conductive materials. Rather than using typical button input or sensors, we connected common organic items such as potatoes, carrots, plants, aluminum foil, and conductive thread to the circuit, enabling them to act as touch-based input. Using code, each item was assigned a unique pitch or tone to produce a working musical device.

What I found especially fascinating about this workshop was the unusual pairing of technology with the use of common physical items. It showed that the creation of interaction and sound systems did not have to be done through the use of highly engineered interfaces but could instead arise from the combination of physical manipulation and the experimentation process. In fact, my group member Deeksha succeeded in playing "Mary Had a Little Lamb" using the vegetative input! This workshop provided me with insight into the world of physical computing and how computational devices may be used for interactive performances.
